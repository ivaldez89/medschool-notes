High-Yield Base Cases - Diverticular Disease, IBD, LGIB, Obstruction & Perianal Pathology

üìê Anatomy Anchors (Test Day Essentials)

Key Segments: Cecum ‚Üí Ascending ‚Üí Transverse ‚Üí Descending ‚Üí Sigmoid ‚Üí Rectum ‚Üí Anal canal

Blood Supply: SMA (cecum ‚Üí mid-transverse) | IMA (splenic flexure ‚Üí sigmoid) | Watershed at splenic flexure

Dentate Line ~2-3cm
Rectum ~12-15cm
Sigmoid ~40cm

üß™ Key Diagnostics

  • CT A/P contrast: Diverticulitis staging, abscess detection
  • Colonoscopy: LGIB source, polypectomy, CRC diagnosis
  • Bleeding scan: Intermittent bleed ‚â•0.5 mL/min
  • Angiography: Brisk bleed ‚â•1 mL/min, angiodysplasia
  • AXR/contrast enema: Volvulus signs (kidney-bean, bird's-beak)
  • Stool toxin: C. difficile diagnosis

üíä Key Medications

  • Cefoxitin: 2g IV q6-8h (broad G-/anaerobe coverage)
  • Pip/Tazo: 3.375-4.5g IV q6-8h (severe diverticulitis)
  • Metronidazole PO: 500mg q8-12h (C. diff)
  • Vancomycin PO: 125mg q6h (severe C. diff)
  • Neostigmine IV: 2mg slow push (Ogilvie - telemetry!)
  • 5-ASA: Maintenance therapy for UC

üéØ High-Yield NBME Traps & Pearls

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Traps:

  • Scoping acute diverticulitis ‚Üí Perforation risk!
  • Calling painless hematochezia "hemorrhoids" ‚Üí Think diverticulosis
  • Treating sigmoid and cecal volvulus same ‚Üí Sigmoid scope, cecal surgery
  • Missing ischemic colitis after AAA ‚Üí LLQ pain + bleeding
  • Assuming UGI can't cause hematochezia ‚Üí NG lavage first
  • Ogilvie managed like SBO ‚Üí Try neostigmine first
  • UC vs Crohn surgery confusion ‚Üí UC curative, Crohn recurs
  • Anal canal SCC sent to APR ‚Üí Nigro protocol first

üíé Must-Know Pearls:

  • Hinchey staging: I=pericolic, II=pelvic, III=purulent, IV=fecal
  • Angiodysplasia: Cecum/ascending, elderly, angiography best
  • Ischemic colitis: Splenic flexure watershed, thumbprinting
  • Polyp malignancy: <1cm ~3%, 1-2cm ~10%, >2cm ~40%
  • CRC survival: I ~90%, II ~75%, III ~50%, IV ~5%
  • Goodsall's rule: Posterior curves, anterior straight
  • Fissure location: Posterior midline typical
üî• Base Case 1: "LLQ pain + fever after nuts & popcorn"
Patient: 62-year-old with constant LLQ pain, fever, anorexia, leukocytosis, localized tenderness
Diagnosis: Acute diverticulitis
Differential: CRC, ischemic colitis, UTI, gynecologic disease, appendicitis (if right-sided)

üéØ Classic Buzzwords:

  • LLQ pain + fever
  • Leukocytosis
  • Pericolic fat stranding on CT
  • History of nuts/seeds

‚ö° Next Best Step:

CT abdomen/pelvis with contrast ‚Üí Stage with Hinchey classification
Don't: Colonoscopy or barium enema in acute setting (perforation risk)

üîÄ Clinical Forks ("What if...?")

  • Uncomplicated (Hinchey 0): Outpatient clear liquids + PO antibiotics, close follow-up
  • Hinchey I-II (localized/pelvic abscess): IV antibiotics + CT-guided drainage
  • Hinchey III-IV (purulent/fecal peritonitis): Urgent OR ‚Üí Hartmann vs primary anastomosis
  • Recurrent episodes: Elective sigmoid resection after acute episode resolves

üí° Learning Takeaways:

  • CT first, stage it, drain abscesses
  • Operate for peritonitis or failed drainage
  • Interval colonoscopy 4-6 weeks later to exclude malignancy
  • Hinchey staging guides management decisions
‚ö†Ô∏è Red Flags: Free air, diffuse peritonitis ‚Üí emergent laparotomy
ü©∏ Base Case 2: "Painless hematochezia in elderly"
Patient: 74-year-old with sudden bright red blood per rectum, orthostasis, no pain
Diagnosis: Lower GI bleeding (likely diverticular bleed)
Differential: Diverticular bleed, angiodysplasia, CRC, ischemic colitis, hemorrhoids, UGI source

üéØ Classic Buzzwords:

  • Painless massive bleeding
  • Bright red blood
  • Hemodynamic instability
  • Elderly patient

‚ö° Next Best Step:

Resuscitate ‚Üí NG lavage (exclude UGI) ‚Üí Colonoscopy for diagnosis ¬± therapy
If brisk (‚â•1 mL/min): Angiography ¬± embolization

üîÄ Clinical Forks ("What if...?")

  • Intermittent bleeding (‚â•0.5 mL/min): Radionuclide bleeding scan ‚Üí target angiography
  • Persistent massive, no source identified: Exploratory laparotomy ¬± subtotal colectomy
  • Angiodysplasia found: Selective mesenteric angiography best for diagnosis/treatment
  • Anticoagulated patient: Reverse if appropriate

üí° Learning Takeaways:

  • Diverticulosis = painless massive bleeding
  • Angiodysplasia = slower, right-sided, elderly
  • Always exclude UGI source first
  • Colonoscopy both diagnostic and therapeutic
‚ö†Ô∏è Red Flags: Hemodynamic instability, ongoing bleeding despite resuscitation
üå™Ô∏è Base Case 3: "Diffuse distention & kidney-bean loop"
Patient: 68-year-old with progressive distention, obstipation, nausea/vomiting
Imaging: Dilated colon, "kidney-bean" loop, no rectal gas
Diagnosis: Sigmoid volvulus
Differential: Cecal volvulus, large-bowel obstruction (CRC), diverticular stricture, Ogilvie syndrome

üéØ Classic Buzzwords:

  • Kidney-bean loop on AXR
  • Bird's-beak on enema
  • Massive sigmoid dilation
  • Elderly/institutional patient

‚ö° Next Best Step:

Sigmoid volvulus: Endoscopic decompression + rectal tube ‚Üí elective resection same admission
Cecal volvulus: Right hemicolectomy (endoscopic decompression unreliable)

üîÄ Clinical Forks ("What if...?")

  • Sigmoid volvulus with peritonitis: Emergent Hartmann procedure
  • Cecal volvulus (young/postpartum): Hypermobile cecum ‚Üí immediate right hemicolectomy
  • Successful decompression: ~50% recurrence rate ‚Üí plan elective resection
  • Signs of strangulation: Don't attempt endoscopic decompression

üí° Learning Takeaways:

  • Sigmoid: scope first, then resect
  • Cecal: straight to surgery
  • High recurrence rate without resection
  • Colon obstruction = surgical emergency
‚ö†Ô∏è Red Flags: Strangulation, peritonitis, failed decompression
üí® Base Case 4: "Massive dilation without a block"
Patient: 77-year-old postop with abdominal distention, minimal pain, decreased bowel sounds
Meds: Opioids & antipsychotic
Imaging: Diffuse colonic dilation, no transition point
Diagnosis: Acute colonic pseudo-obstruction (Ogilvie syndrome)
Differential: Mechanical large-bowel obstruction, toxic megacolon, volvulus

üéØ Classic Buzzwords:

  • Pseudo-obstruction
  • Postoperative state
  • Opioids/anticholinergics
  • No mechanical block

‚ö° Next Best Step:

NG + rectal tube ‚Üí stop offending drugs ‚Üí correct electrolytes
If not improving: IV neostigmine (monitored setting) OR colonoscopic decompression

üîÄ Clinical Forks ("What if...?")

  • Peritoneal signs develop: Emercent laparotomy (perforation)
  • Neostigmine contraindicated: Use colonoscopic decompression
  • Cecal diameter >12 cm: High perforation risk ‚Üí urgent decompression
  • Multiple medical comorbidities: Conservative management first

üí° Learning Takeaways:

  • Treat the physiology, not a mechanical block
  • Neostigmine requires telemetry (bradycardia risk)
  • Autonomic imbalance from severe illness
  • Don't operate unless peritonitis
‚ö†Ô∏è Red Flags: Peritonitis, perforation, cecal diameter >12 cm
ü´Ä Base Case 5: "Crampy pain + hematochezia after AAA repair"
Patient: 70-year-old, day 1 postop AAA repair, now LLQ crampy pain and bloody diarrhea
Diagnosis: Ischemic colitis
Differential: Infectious colitis, IBD flare, diverticulitis, C. difficile colitis

üéØ Classic Buzzwords:

  • Post-AAA repair
  • Thumbprinting on imaging
  • Watershed at splenic flexure
  • Crampy LLQ pain

‚ö° Next Best Step:

AXR/CT: Thumbprinting, pneumatosis ‚Üí Colonoscopy (pale mucosa, petechiae)
Mild: IV fluids + observation

üîÄ Clinical Forks ("What if...?")

  • Mild (observation only): IV fluids, bowel rest, serial exams
  • Moderate (fever/‚ÜëWBC): Add IV broad-spectrum antibiotics
  • Severe/peritoneal signs: Exploratory laparotomy with colostomy/resection
  • Pneumatosis/portal air: Consider transmural ischemia

üí° Learning Takeaways:

  • Watershed vulnerability at splenic flexure
  • Pain more insidious than small bowel ischemia
  • IMA thromboembolism or low-flow state
  • Most cases resolve with conservative management
‚ö†Ô∏è Red Flags: Severe pain, acidosis, peritonitis
ü¶† Base Case 6: "Profuse diarrhea after clindamycin"
Patient: 52-year-old post-dental procedure on clindamycin, now watery diarrhea, cramps
Diagnosis: Pseudomembranous colitis (C. difficile)
Differential: Viral gastroenteritis, food poisoning, other antibiotic-associated diarrhea, IBD

üéØ Classic Buzzwords:

  • Recent antibiotics
  • Pseudomembranes on scope
  • Profuse watery diarrhea
  • Contact isolation needed

‚ö° Next Best Step:

Stool toxin ‚Üí Endoscopy if uncertain ‚Üí Stop culprit antibiotic
Treatment: PO metronidazole or PO vancomycin (IV metro if NPO)

üîÄ Clinical Forks ("What if...?")

  • Mild disease: PO metronidazole 500mg q8h
  • Severe disease: PO vancomycin 125mg q6h
  • NPO/ileus: IV metronidazole + consider rectal vancomycin
  • Recurrence (~20%): PO vancomycin with taper

üí° Learning Takeaways:

  • Dysbiosis leads to toxin production
  • Contact isolation essential
  • PO vancomycin doesn't cause systemic levels
  • ~20% recurrence rate
‚ö†Ô∏è Red Flags: Toxic megacolon, fulminant colitis
üîÑ Base Case 7: "Bloody diarrhea vs perianal fistulas"
Patient A (UC): 28-year-old with continuous rectal bleeding, mucus, urgency; lead-pipe colon
Patient B (Crohn): 26-year-old with skip lesions, aphthous ulcers, perianal fistulas
Diagnosis: Inflammatory bowel disease (UC vs Crohn's)
Differential: Infectious colitis, ischemic colitis, radiation colitis, medication-induced colitis

üéØ UC vs Crohn:

  • UC: Continuous, mucosal, rectum ‚Üí proximal
  • Crohn: Skip lesions, transmural, mouth-to-anus
  • UC: Lead-pipe colon
  • Crohn: Cobblestoning, fistulas

‚ö° Next Best Step:

UC: Sigmoidoscopy/biopsy ‚Üí 5-ASA/steroids ‚Üí Proctocolectomy curative
Crohn: Endoscopy + imaging ‚Üí medical therapy ‚Üí bowel-sparing surgery

üîÄ Clinical Forks ("What if...?")

  • UC refractory to medical therapy: Total proctocolectomy (end ileostomy or J-pouch)
  • Crohn complications: Strictureplasty/segmental resections (preserve bowel)
  • UC toxic megacolon: Emergency colectomy
  • Long-standing UC: Increased carcinoma risk ‚Üí surveillance

üí° Learning Takeaways:

  • UC surgery is curative (proctocolectomy)
  • Crohn recurs ‚Üí bowel-sparing approach
  • UC: continuous mucosal disease
  • Crohn: transmural skip lesions
‚ö†Ô∏è Red Flags: Toxic megacolon, perforation, malignant transformation
üéóÔ∏è Base Case 8: "Positive FIT vs iron-deficiency anemia"
Patient: 66-year-old with microcytic anemia and fatigue
Right-sided: Occult bleeding/anemia
Left-sided: Obstruction/"apple-core" lesion
Diagnosis: Colorectal carcinoma
Differential: Polyps, IBD, diverticular disease, hemorrhoids, other GI malignancies

üéØ Classic Patterns:

  • Right colon: Iron deficiency anemia
  • Left colon: Change in caliber, obstruction
  • Progressive symptoms
  • Weight loss, fatigue

‚ö° Next Best Step:

Colonoscopy + biopsy ‚Üí CT chest/abd/pelvis + CEA ‚Üí Oncologic resection per location
Staging: TNM system guides adjuvant therapy

üîÄ Clinical Forks ("What if...?")

  • Right colon/cecum: Right hemicolectomy
  • Left/sigmoid: Left hemicolectomy or sigmoid colectomy
  • Proximal rectum: LAR with TME
  • Distal rectum: APR (permanent colostomy)
  • Stage III disease: Adjuvant 5-FU based chemotherapy
  • Rectal cancer: Neoadjuvant chemoradiation (5-FU radiosensitizer)

üí° Learning Takeaways:

  • Adenoma-carcinoma sequence: APC‚ÜíKRAS‚ÜíDCC‚Üíp53
  • Screening starts at 50 (multiple options)
  • Right = bleeding, Left = obstruction
  • 5-year survival: Stage I ~90%, II ~75%, III ~50%, IV ~5%
‚ö†Ô∏è Red Flags: Perforation, obstruction, late presentation
üî∫ Base Case 9: "Painful defecation with blood streaks"
Fissure: 32-year-old with severe anal pain during BMs, bright red blood
Abscess: 40-year-old with tender erythematous perianal mass, fever
Hemorrhoids: Thrombosed purple anal nodule
Diagnosis: Perianal disease (fissure/abscess/hemorrhoids)
Differential: Crohn's disease, STIs, malignancy, pilonidal disease, hidradenitis suppurativa

üéØ Key Features:

  • Fissure: Posterior midline, hypertonic sphincter
  • Abscess: Fluctuant mass, cryptoglandular origin
  • Hemorrhoids: Above/below dentate line
  • Goodsall's rule for fistula tracts

‚ö° Next Best Step:

Fissure: Fiber, sitz baths, topical nifedipine/nitroglycerin ‚Üí LIS if refractory
Abscess: I&D immediately ‚Üí evaluate for fistula later

üîÄ Clinical Forks ("What if...?")

  • Thrombosed external hemorrhoid <48h pain: Excision
  • Internal hemorrhoids I-III: Rubber band ligation above dentate
  • Grade IV hemorrhoids: Excision
  • Fistula develops after abscess: Unroof tract ¬± seton placement
  • Atypical fissure location: Consider Crohn's, STI, malignancy
  • Pilonidal disease: I&D acutely, hair removal chronically

üí° Learning Takeaways:

  • Posterior fissure = typical (anterior concerning)
  • Don't delay I&D for abscesses
  • Goodsall's rule: posterior tracts curve, anterior go straight
  • Dentate line divides internal/external hemorrhoids
‚ö†Ô∏è Red Flags: Atypical location, sepsis, horseshoe abscess
‚ö° Base Case 10: "Perianal mass - biopsy shows SCC"
Patient: Perianal mass, biopsy shows SCC of anal canal
Diagnosis: Anal canal squamous cell carcinoma
Differential: Anal margin SCC, adenocarcinoma, melanoma, condyloma, hemorrhoids

üéØ Key Features:

  • Anal canal: Epidermoid carcinoma
  • Anal margin: Often small/superficial
  • HPV association
  • Better prognosis than rectal adenocarcinoma

‚ö° Next Best Step:

Nigro protocol first-line: 5-FU + mitomycin C + external beam radiation
APR reserved for: Treatment failures/recurrence

üîÄ Clinical Forks ("What if...?")

  • Anal margin tumors (small/superficial): Wide local excision ¬± adjuvant therapy
  • Failed chemoradiation: Salvage APR
  • Anal melanoma: Poor prognosis, often requires APR
  • Positive margins after WLE: Re-excision or adjuvant radiation

üí° Learning Takeaways:

  • Organ-preserving approach for anal canal SCC
  • Nigro protocol: 5-FU + mitomycin + radiation
  • APR for failures, not first-line
  • Prognosis: anal margin ~80%, canal SCC ~50%

Final Wrap - Key Takeaways

Diverticulitis Protocol

CT first ‚Üí stage by Hinchey ‚Üí drain abscesses ‚Üí operate for peritonitis ‚Üí scope at 4-6 weeks

LGIB Workflow

Resuscitate ‚Üí exclude UGI ‚Üí colonoscopy ‚Üí scan/angio ‚Üí surgery if needed

Volvulus Management

Sigmoid: Endoscopic decompression first
Cecal: Straight to hemicolectomy

Ogilvie Treatment

Neostigmine or colonoscopic decompression
Operate only for peritonitis

IBD Surgery

UC: Proctocolectomy curative
Crohn: Bowel-sparing approach

CRC Patterns

Right: Anemia, occult bleeding
Left: Obstruction, apple-core lesion

Perianal Disease

Fissure: Posterior midline, topical therapy
Abscess: I&D immediately

Anal Cancer

Nigro protocol first-line for SCC
APR for failures only

Remember: Large bowel emergencies need rapid recognition and appropriate intervention - from bleeding to obstruction to perforation!
